# Transparency and Record-Keeping

If governance is about knowing what AI your business uses, transparency is about being willing to tell people. And record-keeping is how you prove it. Together, they're your safety net — both for building trust and for covering yourself if something goes wrong.

The good news: NZ doesn't have a mandatory AI disclosure law. The less-good news: the government strongly recommends transparency, and the direction of travel is clear. Businesses that build good habits now won't be scrambling later.

## The MBIE AI Transparency Checklist

MBIE's Responsible AI Guidance for Business (July 2025) includes a practical transparency checklist. It's voluntary, but it's what regulators and customers will increasingly expect. Here's what it covers, translated for small business:

**Tell people what AI you use and why.**
You don't need to publish a technical whitepaper. A simple statement on your website or in your terms of service is enough: "We use AI tools to help with [customer service / content creation / data analysis]. Here's how we keep your information safe."

**Explain how AI-assisted decisions are made.**
If AI influences a decision that affects a customer — say, a recommendation, a risk assessment, or a pricing calculation — they should know AI was involved. This is especially important when the decision has a material impact on someone.

**Be clear about human involvement.**
Customers feel better knowing a human is in the loop. If your AI drafts a response but a team member reviews it before sending, say so. "AI-assisted, human-reviewed" is a powerful trust statement.

**Describe how you handle data.**
What data does your AI process? Where is it stored? How long do you keep it? This doesn't need to be exhaustive — just honest.

> **Tip:** You can combine your AI transparency statement with your existing privacy policy. Add a section on AI use rather than creating a separate document.

## When and How to Disclose AI Use

There's no single rule for when you must tell customers AI is involved, because there's no mandatory AI disclosure law in NZ yet. But here are practical guidelines:

| Situation | Should You Disclose? | How |
|-----------|---------------------|-----|
| AI drafts customer emails | Yes | Mention in your communications policy or footer |
| AI chatbot handles enquiries | Yes — customers should know they're talking to AI | Label the chatbot clearly |
| AI generates marketing content | Optional but recommended | Consider a note like "Created with AI assistance" |
| AI analyses internal data | No disclosure needed externally | Document it internally |
| AI helps with hiring or HR decisions | Yes — strong ethical expectation | Tell candidates how AI is used |
| AI generates financial reports | Document internally | Note AI involvement in report methodology |

The key principle: **the higher the stakes for the person affected, the stronger the expectation of disclosure.**

## What Records to Keep

Record-keeping isn't about creating audit trails for the sake of it. It's about being able to answer the question: "What AI does your business use, and how do you manage it?" — whether that question comes from a customer, a regulator, an insurer, or yourself six months from now.

Here's what to record:

**Your AI tool register.** A central list of every AI tool in use. This is the backbone of your record-keeping. Use the template below.

**Data processing records.** For each tool, note what types of data it processes, where that data goes, and who has access.

**Incident records.** If something goes wrong — an AI produces harmful output, data is leaked, a customer complains — document what happened, what you did about it, and what you changed.

**Decision logs.** For important AI-assisted decisions (especially ones affecting customers or staff), keep a brief note of what the AI recommended, what the human decided, and why.

**Review records.** Each time you review your AI tools or policies, note the date and any changes made.

## Your AI Register Template

A spreadsheet with these columns will cover most small businesses:

| Tool Name | Purpose | Data Types Processed | Users | Account Type | Data Location | Last Reviewed |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|-------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| ChatGPT | Customer comms drafting | Customer names, enquiry details | Sarah, Mike | Business (Team) | US (OpenAI servers) | Mar 2026 |
| Xero (JAX) | Financial categorisation | Transaction data, supplier names | Sarah | Paid (Business) | AU/NZ | Mar 2026 |
| Canva AI | Marketing graphics | No personal data | Mike | Pro | US/AU | Mar 2026 |

> **Important:** Review this register at least every six months. AI tools change their terms, features, and data practices regularly. A tool that was fine in January might have changed its policies by July.

## Communicating AI Use to Customers

Telling customers you use AI doesn't have to be awkward. Most people are fine with it — they just want honesty and confidence that their data is handled carefully.

**On your website:** Add a short section to your privacy policy or create a brief "How We Use AI" page. Keep it plain-English. Something like:

*"We use AI-powered tools to help us work efficiently and serve you better. This includes drafting communications, analysing data, and creating marketing content. We use business-grade AI platforms with data protection agreements in place. A team member always reviews AI-assisted work before it reaches you. We never put sensitive personal information into AI tools that don't meet our security standards."*

**In customer interactions:** If a customer asks whether they're talking to AI (e.g., via a chatbot), tell them. "You're chatting with our AI assistant. A team member can take over if you prefer." Simple.

**In proposals and reports:** If AI significantly contributed to a deliverable, consider a note: "This report was prepared with AI assistance and reviewed by [Name]." This is becoming standard practice in professional services.

## Internal Documentation: Decision Logs

For AI-assisted decisions that matter — ones affecting customers, finances, or staff — keep a brief decision log. This protects you if a decision is ever questioned.

A decision log entry might look like:

| Date | Decision | AI Tool Used | AI Recommendation | Human Decision | Reason |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|--------|
| 12 Mar 2026 | Customer complaint response | Claude | Suggested partial refund and apology | Full refund and apology | Customer is long-term — relationship value |
| 10 Mar 2026 | Blog content direction | ChatGPT | Five topic suggestions | Chose topics 2 and 4 | Best fit for upcoming campaign |

You don't need to log every AI interaction — just the ones where the outcome matters.

## A Spreadsheet Is Fine

Let's be clear: for most small businesses, a well-maintained spreadsheet is all you need for record-keeping. You don't need governance software, a database, or a compliance platform. A Google Sheet or Excel file with your AI register, incident log, and decision log — saved somewhere your team can access — is perfectly adequate.

The goal isn't perfection. It's having a record you can point to when someone asks, "How do you manage your AI use?"

## Key Takeaways

- NZ has no mandatory AI disclosure law, but the MBIE guidance strongly recommends transparency — and the trend is heading that direction.
- Tell customers about AI use, especially when it affects them directly. Higher stakes mean stronger disclosure expectations.
- Keep an AI register: every tool, its purpose, the data it processes, who uses it, and when you last reviewed it.
- Log AI-assisted decisions that matter — especially those affecting customers, staff, or finances.
- Record any AI incidents and how you responded.
- A spreadsheet is a perfectly good record-keeping system for a small business. Start simple and build from there.
