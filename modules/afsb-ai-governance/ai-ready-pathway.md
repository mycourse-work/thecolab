# The AI Ready Pathway

The AI Forum NZ has built a free governance framework that gives businesses a clear path from "we should probably do something about AI governance" to "we've got this under control." It's called the AI Governance Framework, and the entry-level pathway is called **AI Ready**.

AI Ready has eight core practices. They're designed for organisations just getting started — which makes them perfect for small businesses. In this lesson, we'll walk through each one and show you what it looks like at small business scale. No jargon, no enterprise-speak.

You can access the full framework free at [aigovernance.aiforum.org.nz](https://aigovernance.aiforum.org.nz).

## Practice 1: Establish Governance Ownership

**What it means:** Someone in your business needs to be the person responsible for AI governance. Not a committee, not "everyone" — one named person.

**Small business version:** If you're a sole trader, that's you. If you have a team, pick the person who's most across your AI tools. This doesn't have to be a formal title — just make it clear who owns the decisions about AI in your business.

Their job is simple: know what AI tools you use, make sure there's a policy, and be the person people come to with questions about AI use.

> **Tip:** In a business with fewer than 20 people, the owner or a senior manager is usually the right choice. Don't overthink it.

## Practice 2: Define Responsible AI Principles

**What it means:** Decide what values guide your AI use. These become the lens through which you assess every AI decision.

**Small business version:** You don't need a formal ethics statement. Three to five short principles are enough. Here are some examples:

- We always check AI outputs before they reach customers.
- We don't put personal or confidential data into free AI tools.
- We're transparent with customers about when AI is involved.
- We treat AI as a tool that supports our team, not one that replaces them.
- We respect customer privacy and Te Tiriti obligations.

Write them down. Put them in your AI policy. Refer to them when you're making decisions about new tools.

## Practice 3: Develop an AI Policy

**What it means:** A written document that sets out the rules for how your business uses AI.

**Small business version:** If you completed Module 5, you've already drafted a workplace AI policy. That policy covers what you're allowed to use, what data is off-limits, and how to handle AI outputs. That IS your AI governance policy — you don't need a separate document.

If you haven't created one yet, start with these essentials:

- **Approved tools:** List the AI tools your business has approved for use.
- **Data rules:** What types of data can go into AI tools, and what can't?
- **Human oversight:** What must be reviewed by a person before it goes to a customer?
- **Reporting:** What should staff do if something goes wrong?

A one-page policy that people actually read is worth more than a 20-page document that sits in a drawer.

## Practice 4: Conduct Risk Assessments

**What it means:** Think through what could go wrong with each AI tool you use, and how likely it is.

**Small business version:** You don't need a formal risk register. For each AI tool, ask yourself three questions:

1. **What's the worst that could happen?** (e.g., customer data leaked, wrong advice given, biased output)
2. **How likely is it?** (unlikely / possible / likely)
3. **What's my plan if it happens?** (e.g., stop using the tool, notify affected customers, switch to manual process)

Here's a simple risk assessment table you can use:

| AI Tool | Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---------|------|------------|--------|------------|
| ChatGPT (business) | Inaccurate customer advice | Possible | Medium | Human review before sending |
| Canva AI | Brand inconsistency | Unlikely | Low | Use brand templates |
| Xero JAX | Wrong categorisation | Possible | Medium | Monthly reconciliation check |
| Free AI chatbot (staff) | Data leak via training | Likely | High | Ban free tools for business data |

> **Important:** Review your risk assessments every six months, or whenever you add a new AI tool. Things change fast in AI — a tool that was safe six months ago might have changed its terms.

## Practice 5: Build an AI System Inventory

**What it means:** Create a list of every AI tool your business uses. If you don't know what you're running, you can't govern it.

**Small business version:** A spreadsheet or even a simple list in a shared document. For each tool, record:

- Tool name and what it does
- Who uses it
- What data it processes
- Whether it's a free or paid account
- When you last reviewed it

This is also your shadow AI detector. Send your team a quick message: "Please reply with every AI tool you use for work, including anything you found on your own." You might be surprised what comes back.

## Practice 6: Build Capability

**What it means:** Make sure your team knows how to use AI well and safely.

**Small business version:** Training doesn't have to mean formal courses (although this course is a great start). A 30-minute team session on your AI policy, sharing prompting tips relevant to your work, including AI tools in onboarding, and a quick monthly catch-up ("What's working? Any new tools we should look at?") will cover it. The AI Forum NZ also offers free e-learning modules at aiforum.org.nz.

## Practice 7: Define Human Oversight Points

**What it means:** Decide where a human must check, review, or approve AI output before it's used.

**Small business version:** Not every AI use needs human oversight. Asking AI to brainstorm blog post ideas? Probably fine. Using AI to draft a legal response to a customer complaint? Definitely needs a human check.

A practical rule of thumb:

| Situation | Human Oversight Needed? |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Internal brainstorming and research | No |
| Drafting internal documents | Light review |
| Customer-facing content | Yes — review before sending |
| Financial calculations or advice | Yes — verify the numbers |
| Legal or compliance matters | Yes — always |
| Decisions about people (hiring, performance) | Yes — always |
| Health or safety information | Yes — always |

Write these into your AI policy so everyone knows the expectations.

## Practice 8: Engage Stakeholders

**What it means:** Involve the people affected by your AI use — your team, your customers, and your community.

**Small business version:** This is simpler than it sounds:

- **Your team:** Ask for their input when setting AI policy. What tools do they find useful? What worries them? People follow rules they helped create.
- **Your customers:** Be upfront about AI use. A line on your website or in your terms of service goes a long way. Most customers are fine with AI — they just want honesty.
- **Your community:** If your business serves a specific community (e.g., Maori, Pasifika, rural), consider whether your AI use aligns with their values and expectations.

## Your AI Ready Checklist

Use this to track your progress. You don't have to do everything at once — tick them off over time.

- [ ] Named a governance owner
- [ ] Written 3-5 responsible AI principles
- [ ] Created (or updated) an AI policy
- [ ] Done a risk assessment for each AI tool
- [ ] Built an AI system inventory (list of all tools)
- [ ] Planned team AI training or shared resources
- [ ] Defined where human oversight is required
- [ ] Communicated with team and customers about AI use

> **Resource:** The full AI Forum NZ governance framework is free at [aigovernance.aiforum.org.nz](https://aigovernance.aiforum.org.nz). It includes templates, maturity assessments, and pathways for growing your governance as your AI use matures.

## Key Takeaways

- The AI Forum NZ's "AI Ready" pathway has eight core practices — all achievable for a small business.
- Start with governance ownership (one named person) and a written AI policy.
- A simple risk assessment table and AI tool inventory can fit in a single spreadsheet.
- Human oversight rules should match the stakes — brainstorming needs less oversight than customer-facing content.
- Involve your team in creating the rules — they're more likely to follow policies they helped shape.
- The full framework is free at aigovernance.aiforum.org.nz — use it as your roadmap.
