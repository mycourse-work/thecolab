# Pilot, Measure, Scale

You've picked your first AI use case. Now you need a plan that doesn't involve crossing your fingers and hoping for the best. The smartest way to implement AI is in three phases: **pilot** it small, **measure** whether it's working, and **scale** it once you know it is.

This approach protects you from wasting money on something that doesn't fit, and gives you the evidence you need to invest further with confidence.

## Designing a Good Pilot

A pilot isn't "let everyone play with ChatGPT for a bit." It's a structured experiment with clear boundaries. Here's how to set one up:

### The Four Elements of a Good Pilot

1. **Pick one team or process** — Don't roll AI out to the whole business at once. Choose a single team, department, or workflow. The smaller the scope, the easier it is to measure.

2. **Set a timeframe** — Four to eight weeks is the sweet spot. Any shorter and you won't have enough data. Any longer and you'll lose momentum.

3. **Define success metrics upfront** — What does "working" look like? Be specific. Time saved per week? Error rate reduction? Customer response speed?

4. **Assign an owner** — Someone needs to be responsible for running the pilot, collecting feedback, and reporting results. In a small team, this might be you.

### Pilot Planning Template

Use this template to plan your first AI pilot:

| Element | Your Plan |
|---------|-----------|
| **Use case** | What specific task will AI handle? |
| **Team/person** | Who will be using the tool? |
| **Tool** | Which AI tool will you use? |
| **Duration** | Start date to end date (4-8 weeks) |
| **Success metric 1** | e.g., "Reduce email drafting time from 45 min to 15 min per day" |
| **Success metric 2** | e.g., "Team satisfaction score of 7+ out of 10" |
| **Success metric 3** | e.g., "No increase in error rate or customer complaints" |
| **Budget** | Monthly tool cost + any training time |
| **Baseline measurement** | Current state before AI (measure this BEFORE you start) |
| **Review date** | When will you assess results? |

> **Important:** Measure your baseline BEFORE you start the pilot. If you don't know how long invoice processing takes today, you can't prove AI made it faster.

## Measuring ROI

ROI isn't just "did we save money?" There are two types of return to track, and they show up at different times.

### Trending ROI (Short-term: Weeks 1-8)

These are the early signals that things are working:

- **Productivity gains** — Are tasks getting done faster? How much time is being saved?
- **Faster turnaround** — Are customers getting responses sooner? Are reports being produced quicker?
- **Employee satisfaction** — Does the team actually like using the tool? Are they finding it helpful or frustrating?
- **Quality indicators** — Is the output as good as (or better than) before?

### Realised ROI (Long-term: 3-24 months)

These are the business outcomes that take longer to materialise:

- **Cost reduction** — Actual money saved through reduced labour hours, fewer errors, or lower operational costs
- **Revenue increase** — More sales, higher conversion rates, or new revenue streams enabled by AI
- **Conversion rates** — Are marketing campaigns performing better? Are leads converting faster?
- **Customer satisfaction** — Are NPS scores improving? Are retention rates going up?

### The ROI Formula

When you're ready to put a number on it:

> **ROI (%) = (Net Benefit / Total Investment) x 100**

Where:
- **Net Benefit** = Value of time saved + cost reductions + revenue gains - tool costs - training costs
- **Total Investment** = Tool subscriptions + setup costs + training time (valued at hourly rate)

**Example:** Your social media content creation pilot saves 5 hours per week. At $50/hour, that's $250/week or $1,000/month. The AI tool costs $30/month, and you spent 4 hours ($200) on initial training.

- Net Benefit (month 1): $1,000 - $30 - $200 = $770
- Total Investment: $30 + $200 = $230
- ROI: ($770 / $230) x 100 = **335%**

That's a first-month return. Over 12 months, the ROI gets dramatically better as the training cost disappears.

> **NZ stat:** Over 25% of NZ organisations report annual benefits exceeding $50,000 from AI, and 75% set up their AI for under $5,000. That's a strong return by any measure.

### Plan for Patience

Be realistic about timeframes. Most businesses need **12-24 months** to see the full ROI of their AI investments. The early wins (time savings, faster workflows) show up quickly. The bigger business outcomes (revenue growth, competitive advantage) take longer.

That's normal. Don't pull the plug after six weeks because you haven't doubled your revenue.

## When to Scale

Your pilot is humming along. Results look good. How do you know it's time to expand?

Look for these signals:

- **Consistent metrics** — Your success metrics have been positive for at least 3-4 weeks, not just a one-off good week
- **Team adoption** — The people using the tool are using it willingly and regularly, not because you're standing over them
- **Workflow integration** — The AI tool has become a natural part of how work gets done, not a separate task
- **Demand from others** — Other team members or departments are asking "can I try that too?"
- **ROI is positive** — Even a modest positive return is a green light

### How to Scale

Scaling doesn't mean "buy enterprise licenses for everyone." It means:

1. **Add users gradually** — Bring on one or two more people at a time
2. **Expand use cases** — Once one workflow is sorted, add another from your Quick Wins list
3. **Document what works** — Create simple guides or templates so new users don't start from zero
4. **Keep measuring** — Track the same metrics at scale that you tracked in the pilot

## When to Pivot

Not every pilot will succeed, and that's genuinely okay. Here's how to recognise when something isn't working:

- **The tool creates more work than it saves** — If people are spending more time fixing AI output than they would doing the task manually, it's not the right fit
- **Team resistance is growing, not shrinking** — Some initial pushback is normal. Sustained, increasing resistance is a signal
- **The metrics aren't moving** — After 4-6 weeks, if there's no measurable improvement, something needs to change
- **The use case was wrong** — Sometimes the tool is fine but the task wasn't a good match. Go back to your Impact/Effort Matrix and try a different use case

> **Tip:** Pivoting isn't failure. Only 15-20% of AI projects succeed on the first attempt across all businesses globally. The businesses that ultimately win are the ones that learn from what didn't work and try again.

### Pivot Options

When a pilot isn't working, you have three choices:

1. **Adjust the tool** — Maybe ChatGPT isn't the right fit but Claude would be. Try a different tool for the same task
2. **Adjust the use case** — Keep the tool but apply it to a different task that's a better match
3. **Adjust the approach** — Maybe the problem is training, not the tool itself. Invest more time in learning how to use it effectively

## Key Takeaways

- A good pilot has four elements: a specific scope, a fixed timeframe (4-8 weeks), clear success metrics, and an owner
- Always measure your baseline before starting — you can't prove improvement without knowing where you started
- Track Trending ROI (productivity, speed, satisfaction) in the short term and Realised ROI (costs, revenue, conversions) over 3-24 months
- Over 25% of NZ organisations report annual AI benefits exceeding $50,000
- Scale when you see consistent positive metrics, genuine team adoption, and positive ROI
- Pivoting isn't failure — only 15-20% of AI projects succeed on the first attempt, so be prepared to iterate
