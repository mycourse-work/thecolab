# Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Here's a sobering number: **only 15-20% of AI projects succeed**. And even fewer scale beyond the pilot stage. That's across all businesses — large and small, globally.

But don't let that put you off. Most failures aren't because AI doesn't work. They're because businesses make the same preventable mistakes over and over. Once you know what those mistakes are, you can sidestep them entirely.

Here are the seven most common pitfalls, what they look like in practice, and exactly how to avoid each one.

## 1. No Clear Objectives

**What it looks like:** "We should be using AI" becomes the entire strategy. Someone buys a ChatGPT subscription, a few people try it for a week, and then it sits unused because nobody knew what problem they were trying to solve.

**NZ example:** A Dunedin professional services firm signed up for Microsoft Copilot licenses across the team because a competitor had. Six months later, usage data showed only two people had opened it more than once. $5,000 spent with nothing to show for it.

**How to avoid it:** Always start with a business problem, not a technology. "Reduce client proposal turnaround from five days to two" is an objective. "Use AI" is not. Refer back to Step 1 of the 7-Step Methodology — every AI initiative needs a specific, measurable goal.

## 2. Overestimating AI Capabilities

**What it looks like:** Expecting AI to be perfect out of the box. Treating it like a magic wand that produces flawless work every time. Getting frustrated when it makes mistakes and abandoning it entirely.

**The reality:** AI is a tool, not a colleague. It generates drafts that need human review. It makes things up sometimes (called "hallucinations"). It doesn't understand your business context unless you explain it clearly. It's brilliant at getting you 80% of the way there — but the last 20% is still your job.

**How to avoid it:** Set expectations clearly with your team from day one: "AI gives us a fast first draft. We review, edit, and improve." Build a review step into every AI-assisted workflow. Never publish, send, or act on AI output without a human checking it first.

> **Tip:** Think of AI as a very fast, very keen intern. It'll work hard and produce something useful, but you wouldn't send an intern's first draft to a client without checking it.

## 3. Neglecting Change Management

**What it looks like:** Buying tools without preparing people. Announcing "we're using AI now" and expecting everyone to figure it out. Wondering why nobody's using the tools you're paying for.

**The stats:** 54% of executives cite resistance as the number one obstacle (PwC). Companies with strong change management are 6x more likely to succeed (Gartner).

**How to avoid it:** We covered this in detail in the previous lesson, but the short version: involve your team early, address concerns honestly, start with a pain point they actually care about, and make AI feel like help — not a mandate. The technology is the easy part. The people are the hard part.

## 4. Poor Data Quality

**What it looks like:** Feeding AI messy, incomplete, or outdated information and being disappointed with the results. Customer databases full of duplicates. Financial records that haven't been reconciled. Processes that exist only in someone's head.

**The principle:** Garbage in, garbage out. This was true before AI and it's even more true now. If your customer list has 500 duplicates, AI will happily generate marketing campaigns for all 500 of them.

**How to avoid it:**
- Clean up your key data before you start — customer lists, product catalogues, financial records
- Start with use cases that don't depend on perfect data (content generation, brainstorming, email drafting)
- Build data hygiene into your regular routines — monthly cleanups, consistent naming conventions, regular deduplication
- Remember: you don't need perfect data to start. You just need to know which data is reliable and which isn't

## 5. Excluding Stakeholders

**What it looks like:** The business owner picks a tool, sets it up, and tells the team to use it — without ever asking what they need or what they think. The people who'll actually use the tool every day had no input into selecting it.

**NZ example:** A Wellington retail business invested in an AI inventory management system chosen entirely by the owner. The warehouse team — who would be using it daily — found it clunky and confusing because it didn't match their workflow. They went back to their spreadsheets within a month.

**How to avoid it:**
- Involve the people who'll use the tool in selecting it
- Ask your team: "What takes you the most time? What do you find most frustrating?"
- Run a short trial with end users before committing to a purchase
- Get feedback early and often — not after you've signed an annual contract

## 6. Ignoring Scalability

**What it looks like:** Picking a tool that works great for one person but can't grow with your business. Free-tier tools that hit limits as soon as you try to add users. Point solutions that don't integrate with your existing systems.

**The cautionary tale — Soul Machines:** This NZ company created world-leading lifelike AI avatars, partnered with P&G, the WHO, and the UN, and raised over $225 million. But the technology scaled faster than the business model could support. They entered receivership despite having genuinely impressive technology. The lesson? Even brilliant AI can fail if the business fundamentals — including scalability — aren't right.

**How to avoid it:**
- Before committing to a tool, ask: "What happens when we need to add five more users? Twenty more?"
- Check integration capabilities — does it work with Xero, your CRM, your email platform?
- Favour tools with clear upgrade paths over tools that lock you into a single tier
- Think about data portability — can you export your data if you need to switch tools later?

## 7. Treating It as "Done"

**What it looks like:** Implementing an AI tool, declaring success, and never revisiting it. Using the same prompts you wrote six months ago. Never updating workflows as the tools improve. Missing out on new features because nobody's paying attention.

**The reality:** AI tools are evolving faster than almost any technology in history. ChatGPT in early 2025 is dramatically more capable than ChatGPT in 2023. What doesn't work today might work brilliantly in six months. What works today might be obsolete in a year.

**How to avoid it:**
- Schedule a quarterly review: "How are our AI tools performing? What's changed?"
- Stay connected to the NZ AI community (AI Forum, industry events, online groups)
- Revisit your prompts and workflows regularly — small tweaks can make a big difference
- Keep an eye on new features and updates from your tool providers
- Reassess use cases that didn't work previously — the technology may have caught up

## The Bottom Line

Most AI failures are people problems and planning problems, not technology problems. The tools work. The question is whether you've set yourself up to use them effectively.

If you take one thing from this lesson, let it be this: **plan before you purchase, involve your team, and start small**. That simple approach will put you ahead of the 80-85% of AI projects that don't make it.

## Key Takeaways

- Only 15-20% of AI projects succeed — but most failures are preventable with proper planning
- The seven common pitfalls: no clear objectives, overestimating capabilities, neglecting change management, poor data quality, excluding stakeholders, ignoring scalability, and treating AI as "done"
- AI is a fast first draft, not a finished product — always include a human review step
- Involve the people who'll actually use the tools in selecting and setting them up
- Soul Machines shows that even brilliant technology can fail without sustainable business fundamentals
- Use the pre-launch checklist before every AI initiative — ten minutes of planning saves months of frustration
- AI tools evolve rapidly — schedule quarterly reviews to keep your implementations current
