You know AI can help your business. You've identified some quick wins. Now the question is: how do you actually roll this out without wasting money, confusing your team, or ending up with a pile of unused subscriptions?

Good news — you don't need to invent this from scratch. There's a proven methodology that works for businesses of all sizes, and we've adapted it specifically for NZ small business. It's seven steps, and they're designed to keep you focused on outcomes, not shiny tools.

Let's walk through each one.

## Step 1: Start with Clear Business Objectives

This is where most businesses go wrong. They start with "we should use AI" instead of "we need to solve this specific problem."

Bad objective: "Let's use AI in our business."

Good objective: "Reduce invoice processing time from 2 hours to 30 minutes per week."

Your objective should be specific, measurable, and tied to something that actually matters to your bottom line or your team's sanity.

**NZ example:** A Tauranga accounting firm didn't say "let's adopt AI." They said "we want to cut the time our team spends categorising receipts by 60%." They implemented Dext, and within a month they'd freed up five hours per week — time that went straight into billable client work.

> **Action:** Write down one specific business problem you want AI to solve. Include a number — a time saved, a cost reduced, or a metric improved.

## Step 2: Choose the Right Tools

Here's a stat that should make this feel less daunting: **72% of NZ businesses choose off-the-shelf AI tools** rather than building custom solutions. That's the right call for most small businesses.

You have three options:

| Approach | Best For | Cost | Examples |
|----------|----------|------|----------|
| Off-the-shelf | Most SMEs | $0-150/month | ChatGPT, Xero JAX, Canva AI |
| Configured/customised | Specific workflows | $500-5,000 setup | Tidio chatbot, Make.com automations |
| Custom-built | Unique, complex needs | $10,000+ | Custom AI models, bespoke systems |

For most NZ small businesses, off-the-shelf tools will get you 80% of the way there. Start there. You can always customise later.

**NZ example:** Bolton Hotel in Wellington didn't build a custom chatbot from scratch. They configured an off-the-shelf solution to create "Bea" — a chatbot that handles guest enquiries 24/7. No massive development budget required.

## Step 3: Get Your Data in Order

AI is only as good as the data you feed it. Before you start, you need to think about two things:

1. **What data can you share with AI tools?** General business info, public content, anonymised metrics — these are usually fine.
2. **What data must stay confidential?** Customer personal information, financial records, employee details, commercially sensitive information — be careful here.

This doesn't mean you need a perfect data strategy before you start. It means you need to know what's okay to put into ChatGPT and what isn't.

**NZ example:** A Christchurch recruitment agency wanted to use AI to write job ads. They could safely share role descriptions and required skills. But they made sure candidate CVs and personal details never went into any AI tool — a smart move under the Privacy Act 2020.

> **Tip:** Create a simple two-column list: "Can share with AI" and "Keep confidential." It doesn't need to be fancy — just clear.

## Step 4: Prioritise High-Impact Use Cases

Remember the Impact/Effort Matrix from Module 4? This is where you use it. List out all the potential AI use cases you've identified, then plot them:

- **Quick Wins** (high impact, low effort) — these go first
- **Strategic Projects** (high impact, high effort) — plan for later
- **Self-Service** (low impact, low effort) — nice to have
- **Deferred** (low impact, high effort) — park these

Don't try to do five things at once. Pick your top one or two Quick Wins and focus on those.

**NZ example:** A Wellington marketing agency mapped out twelve potential AI use cases. They prioritised two: drafting social media content (quick win) and generating first-draft blog posts (quick win). They deliberately parked "AI-powered client reporting dashboards" for later — high impact but high effort.

## Step 6: Encourage Adoption Through Training

The tools don't matter if your team won't use them. The key here is framing: AI is a **facilitator**, not a replacement.

Practical ways to encourage adoption:

- Show the team how AI handles the tasks they find tedious (data entry, report formatting, email drafting)
- Give people time to experiment — even 30 minutes a week to play with a tool
- Celebrate early wins publicly ("Sarah used Claude to cut our monthly report time in half!")
- Don't mandate — invite. Let people discover the value themselves

**NZ example:** PwC NZ learned this the hard way. When they rolled out GenAI firm-wide, leadership buy-in and structured change management were essential. The firms that just bought licenses and sent a link got nowhere.

> **Remember:** Only 24% of NZ workers have received any AI training. Investing even a small amount in upskilling puts you ahead of most businesses.

## Step 7: Manage Costs and Expectations

Two rules here:

1. **Start with limited licenses.** Don't buy a 20-seat subscription when three people will actually use it. Most AI tools offer monthly plans — start with one or two seats and expand once you see results.

2. **Set realistic timeframes.** AI won't transform your business overnight. Plan for 4-8 weeks to see initial results from a pilot, and 12-24 months to see meaningful ROI.

The good news? 75% of NZ organisations report AI setup costs under $5,000. This isn't a massive capital investment — it's a manageable, incremental spend.

**NZ example:** A Hamilton trades business started with a single ChatGPT Plus subscription ($30/month) for the office manager. After two months of proven time savings, they added two more seats. Total investment in month one: $30. Not exactly bet-the-farm territory.

## Putting It All Together

These seven steps aren't a one-time exercise. Think of them as a cycle — you'll come back to them every time you want to add a new AI capability. The difference is that each time around, you'll be faster and more confident.

The businesses that succeed with AI aren't the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones with the clearest objectives, the most practical expectations, and the willingness to start small.

## Key Takeaways

- Start with a specific business objective, not a technology — "reduce X by Y%" beats "use AI"
- 72% of NZ businesses use off-the-shelf tools — you probably should too
- Sort your data into "can share" and "keep confidential" before you start
- Prioritise Quick Wins from your Impact/Effort Matrix — don't try to do everything at once
- NZ-based AI consultants and government programmes can help fill gaps
- Frame AI as a facilitator for your team, not a replacement — invest in training
- Start with limited licenses and realistic timeframes — 75% of NZ businesses set up AI for under $5,000
