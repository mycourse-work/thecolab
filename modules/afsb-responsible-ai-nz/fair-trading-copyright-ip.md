# Fair Trading, Copyright, and IP

AI can write your marketing copy, generate images, and draft business documents in seconds. But who's legally responsible for what it produces? And who owns it? This lesson covers the two big areas you need to know about: fair trading obligations and intellectual property.

## Fair Trading Act — You're Responsible, Not the AI

Here's the key principle: **if you publish it, you own it.** The Fair Trading Act prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct in trade. It doesn't matter that AI wrote your ad copy, product description, or social media post. If it goes out under your business name, you're legally responsible for its accuracy.

This means:

- **You must have reasonable grounds for any claims.** If your AI writes "We offer the lowest prices in Wellington" or "Our product is 100% organic," you need evidence to back that up.
- **Don't blindly publish AI-generated content.** AI confidently produces text that sounds authoritative but may be completely wrong. Always fact-check marketing claims before publishing.
- **Testimonials and reviews must be genuine.** Using AI to generate fake customer reviews is misleading conduct under the Act.

### The Penalties Are Increasing

From 2026, the maximum fine for breaching the Fair Trading Act is jumping from $600,000 to **$5 million** (or three times the commercial gain from the breach, whichever is higher). That's a serious increase, and it makes fact-checking your AI outputs more important than ever.

> **Warning:** "The AI wrote it" is not a defence. The Commerce Commission has made it clear that businesses are responsible for all claims they make, regardless of how they were generated.

### Practical Examples

| AI Output | The Problem | What To Do |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| "Our service is the best in NZ" | Unsubstantiated superiority claim | Remove or add qualifying language like "one of" |
| "Guaranteed results in 30 days" | Must have evidence this is consistently achievable | Only claim what you can prove |
| "As recommended by [celebrity]" | AI may fabricate endorsements | Never publish celebrity/expert endorsements without verification |
| "100% satisfaction rate" | Statistical claims need evidence | Use real, verifiable data |

## Copyright — NZ Is Unique

Here's something that surprises many people: New Zealand is one of very few countries that **does** grant copyright protection to computer-generated works. Under Section 5(2)(a) of the Copyright Act 1994, a "computer-generated" work is one created in circumstances where there's no human author.

### Who Owns AI-Generated Content?

The copyright belongs to the person who made the "arrangements necessary for the creation of the work." In practice, this means the person who:

- Chose to use the AI tool
- Crafted the prompts
- Selected, edited, and refined the output
- Made creative decisions about the final result

The more active human involvement you have, the stronger your copyright claim. Simply typing "write me a blog post about plumbing" gives you a weaker claim than providing detailed prompts, specifying structure, editing extensively, and combining AI output with your own writing.

> **Tip:** Keep records of your prompts, editing process, and creative decisions. If you ever need to prove copyright ownership, this trail of human involvement will be valuable.

### But Check the Terms of Service

There's a catch. Each AI tool has its own terms of service that may affect your IP rights:

- **ChatGPT (OpenAI):** Assigns output rights to the user on paid plans. Free tier is less clear.
- **Claude (Anthropic):** Users own their outputs under commercial terms.
- **Midjourney:** Paid subscribers own their outputs, but Midjourney retains a licence to use them.
- **Canva AI:** You own what you create, subject to Canva's content licence agreement.

**Always read the terms of service** for any AI tool you use for business. The terms may vary between free and paid tiers, and they change regularly.

## No Text and Data Mining Exception

Unlike the UK (which has proposed one), New Zealand has **no text and data mining exception** in copyright law. This means:

- If you feed copyrighted material into an AI tool, you could be infringing copyright.
- Don't upload other people's copyrighted content (articles, books, images) into AI tools for analysis or rewriting without permission.
- AI-generated content might inadvertently reproduce elements of copyrighted works the AI was trained on — another reason to review and edit outputs carefully.

This is an area where the law is still developing internationally, but right now, NZ's position is clear: existing copyright protections apply.

## Practical IP Tips for Your Business

### Protecting Your AI-Generated Content

1. **Document your process.** Keep records of prompts, iterations, and editing decisions. This establishes the human involvement that strengthens your copyright claim.
2. **Edit and add original material.** Don't publish raw AI output. Add your expertise, local knowledge, and unique perspective.
3. **Use business accounts.** Enterprise terms typically give you clearer IP ownership than free tiers.
4. **Register trademarks separately.** AI-generated brand names or logos can be trademarked, but go through the normal IPONZ registration process.

### Avoiding IP Problems

1. **Don't upload others' copyrighted work into AI tools** for analysis or rewriting.
2. **Run image searches** on AI-generated images before using them commercially. AI image generators can produce outputs that closely resemble existing copyrighted images.
3. **Be cautious with AI-generated code.** It may reproduce open-source code with licence obligations.
4. **Don't use AI to copy a competitor's style, brand, or content.** Even if the AI creates something "original," if it's clearly derived from a competitor's work, you could face issues.

> **Important:** If you're creating significant IP with AI — a product, brand identity, or substantial written work — it's worth getting specific legal advice. The law in this area is evolving quickly, and the stakes can be high.

### When to Get Legal Advice

Consider talking to a lawyer who understands IP and technology if:

- You're building a product or service that relies heavily on AI-generated content
- You're using AI to create a brand identity (name, logo, tagline)
- You're in an industry with specific regulatory requirements (health, finance, legal)
- You plan to licence or sell AI-generated content
- You receive a copyright complaint about AI-generated content

A one-off consultation is far cheaper than defending a legal claim. Many NZ law firms now have technology specialists who understand AI-specific IP issues.

## Key Takeaways

- Under the Fair Trading Act, you're responsible for every claim your business makes, including AI-generated content. Penalties are rising to $5M from 2026.
- New Zealand uniquely protects copyright in computer-generated works. The more human involvement you have, the stronger your claim.
- Always check the AI tool's terms of service — IP ownership varies by platform and pricing tier.
- NZ has no text and data mining exception, so be careful about feeding copyrighted material into AI tools.
- Document your creative process, edit AI outputs, and get legal advice for significant IP decisions.
