# Te Tiriti, Maori Data Sovereignty, and AI

AI doesn't exist in a cultural vacuum. In Aotearoa New Zealand, the way we develop and use technology is shaped by Te Tiriti o Waitangi and the rights and interests of tangata whenua. This lesson explains why that matters for your business and what respectful, culturally aware AI use looks like.

> **What you'll learn:** Explain the six principles of Maori data sovereignty (Te Mana Raraunga) and their relevance to AI | Identify when data may be culturally sensitive regardless of its legal status | Apply the Algorithm Charter's transparency and partnership commitments to your AI use | Test your AI tools for cultural bias, including handling of te reo Maori names and place names

## Why This Matters

The AI Forum NZ has noted that trust concerns around AI are "particularly acute among Maori and Pasifika communities." There are good reasons for this. Historically, data about Maori has been collected, controlled, and used by others — often without meaningful Maori input or benefit. AI amplifies these concerns because it can process and make decisions about people at scale.

If your business serves Maori customers, operates in communities with significant Maori populations, or handles data that relates to Maori, understanding these issues isn't optional — it's part of doing business well in Aotearoa.

Even if you think this doesn't directly apply to your business, understanding data sovereignty principles makes you a better, more thoughtful user of AI technology.

## Te Mana Raraunga — Maori Data Sovereignty

Te Mana Raraunga is the Maori Data Sovereignty Network, established to advance Maori rights and interests in data. They've developed six principles that guide how data about Maori should be handled. These aren't just theoretical — they're practical guidelines that increasingly inform government policy and business practice.

| Principle | Te Reo Maori | What It Means |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Authority** | Rangatiratanga | Maori have the right to govern the collection, ownership, and use of data about Maori. This includes deciding how AI systems use that data. |
| **Connections** | Whakapapa | Data about Maori is connected to people, places, and history. It has genealogy and context that matters. Stripping data from its context — which AI often does — can be harmful. |
| **Relationships** | Whanaungatanga | Data should be used in ways that nurture relationships and serve collective wellbeing, not just individual or commercial interests. |
| **Unity** | Kotahitanga | Data about Maori should be used in ways that benefit Maori collectively, supporting shared aspirations rather than fragmenting communities. |
| **Guardianship** | Kaitiakitanga | Those who hold data about Maori have a responsibility to care for it and protect it. This is stewardship, not just security. |
| **Reciprocity** | Manaakitanga | The use of data about Maori should provide benefit back to Maori communities. It's about mutual respect and shared value. |

## Maori Algorithmic Sovereignty

Beyond data, there's a growing conversation about algorithmic sovereignty — the idea that tikanga (customary practices), kawa (protocols), and matauranga Maori (knowledge) should be considered in how AI systems are designed and used.

One important concept is the distinction between **tapu** (sacred, restricted) and **noa** (ordinary, unrestricted) as it applies to data. Some information is culturally sensitive and shouldn't be freely shared, processed by AI, or made publicly available — regardless of what a privacy policy might technically allow.

This might include:

- Whakapapa (genealogical) information
- Knowledge connected to specific wahi tapu (sacred places)
- Cultural practices and traditions belonging to specific iwi or hapu
- Health and social data that could be used to profile Maori communities

> **Important:** Just because data is legally available doesn't mean it's culturally appropriate to feed it into an AI system. Thinking about the cultural dimensions of data is part of using AI responsibly in Aotearoa.

## The Algorithm Charter for Aotearoa NZ

New Zealand was the first country in the world to create an Algorithm Charter — a commitment by government agencies to use algorithms (including AI) transparently and fairly. Signed by over 20 agencies in July 2020, it includes six commitments:

1. **Transparency** — Being clear about how algorithms are used and what data they rely on
2. **Partnership** — Engaging with communities, including Maori, in algorithm design
3. **People** — Putting people at the centre, with human oversight of significant decisions
4. **Data** — Using quality data and addressing bias
5. **Privacy, Ethics, and Human Rights** — Respecting these in algorithm development
6. **Human Oversight** — Maintaining meaningful human control over algorithmic decisions

While the Charter applies to government agencies, it sets expectations that increasingly influence the private sector too. Customers and communities expect businesses to operate with similar principles.

## What This Means for Your Business

You don't need to be an expert in te reo Maori or tikanga to be respectful. Here's what practical, culturally aware AI use looks like for a small business.

### If You Serve Maori Customers or Communities

- **Be thoughtful about what data you collect and how AI processes it.** Ask yourself: would the people this data is about be comfortable with how it's being used?
- **Avoid using AI to make automated decisions** about Maori customers without human oversight — particularly for credit, services, or access decisions.
- **Be aware of bias in AI.** Most AI tools are trained primarily on English-language, Western data. They may not accurately represent Maori perspectives, te reo Maori, or NZ cultural context.

### If You Handle Data About Maori

- **Consider whether the data has cultural significance** beyond its commercial value.
- **Don't aggregate or profile communities** without their knowledge and input.
- **Be cautious about sharing data with overseas AI platforms** if it includes culturally sensitive information.

### Building Trust

- **Ask, don't assume.** If you're unsure whether your AI use might affect Maori customers or communities, ask. Engage with local iwi, Maori business networks, or cultural advisors.
- **Be transparent.** Tell people how you use AI and what data it processes. This builds trust with all customers, but it's especially important for communities with historical reasons to be cautious.
- **Look for opportunities to give back.** If AI helps your business benefit from data about Maori communities, consider how those communities can share in that value.

> **Tip:** The MBIE Responsible AI Guidance for Business (July 2025) includes specific sections on Te Tiriti considerations and is free to download at mbie.govt.nz.

## Avoiding Bias in AI

AI systems can reflect and amplify existing biases. In an NZ context, this might look like:

- AI customer service tools that don't handle te reo Maori names or place names correctly
- Predictive tools trained on data that underrepresents Maori, leading to inaccurate outcomes
- Marketing AI that relies on stereotypes or fails to reflect NZ's cultural diversity
- Hiring tools that inadvertently screen out candidates based on culturally biased criteria

**Practical step:** Test your AI tools with diverse inputs. Do they handle Maori names with macrons correctly? Do they produce culturally appropriate content for your NZ audience? If not, that's something to address.

## Key Takeaways

- Te Tiriti o Waitangi and Maori data sovereignty are relevant to how businesses use AI in Aotearoa — this isn't just a government concern.
- Te Mana Raraunga's six principles (Rangatiratanga, Whakapapa, Whanaungatanga, Kotahitanga, Kaitiakitanga, Manaakitanga) provide a practical framework for respectful data use.
- Some data is culturally sensitive regardless of its legal status — think about tapu and noa dimensions.
- NZ's Algorithm Charter sets expectations for transparency and fairness that extend beyond government.
- Be thoughtful, be transparent, test for bias, and engage with communities rather than making assumptions.
- The MBIE Responsible AI Guidance includes Te Tiriti-specific considerations for businesses.
