# Insurance, Cyber Risk, and Biometrics

AI creates new opportunities — and new risks. One in three NZ SMEs experienced a cyber-attack in the six months from April to September 2024. Meanwhile, 43% of business leaders say their biggest cyber risk is employees accidentally exposing data through AI tools. This lesson covers what you need to know about protecting your business.

## The Cyber Risk Landscape for NZ Small Business

Let's be honest about the risks. The numbers tell a clear story:

- **1 in 3 NZ SMEs** experienced a cyber-attack in a recent six-month period
- **43% of business leaders** identify accidental employee data exposure via AI as their top cyber concern
- **81% of small businesses** have suffered a breach at some point
- **52% of businesses** say shadow AI (unapproved tool use) is a problem

AI doesn't necessarily create new cyber threats, but it does create new ways for data to leave your business. Every time someone pastes confidential information into an AI tool, that data is travelling to an external server. If it's a free tool, it may be used to train the AI model — meaning your data becomes part of the system.

> **Warning:** Free AI tools are the highest risk. They typically have weaker data protection, may use your inputs for training, and offer no contractual guarantees about how your data is handled.

## Simple Security Practices

You don't need an IT department to get the basics right. These practices significantly reduce your risk.

### Data Handling

- **Never paste confidential data into free AI tools.** Use business/enterprise accounts with proper data agreements.
- **Don't upload customer files, financial records, or sensitive documents** to general-purpose AI tools.
- **Anonymise data** before using it with AI. Remove names, addresses, and identifiers when you're analysing trends or patterns.
- **Use business accounts with data protection agreements.** ChatGPT Business, Claude Team, and Microsoft Copilot all offer enterprise-grade data protection that free tiers don't.

### Access and Authentication

- **Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA)** on all AI tools and business accounts. This is the single most effective security measure you can take.
- **Use unique passwords** for each AI platform — never reuse passwords.
- **Control who has access** to AI tools with business data. Not everyone needs access to everything.
- **Review access regularly.** When someone leaves, remove their access to AI tools immediately.

### Monitoring

- **Know which AI tools your team is using.** The shadow AI problem starts when you don't know what tools are in play.
- **Check AI tool settings.** Many tools have privacy and data settings you can configure — look for options to opt out of model training.
- **Keep an eye on what data is being shared.** Occasional spot-checks help maintain awareness.

## Biometric Processing Privacy Code 2025

This is a newer piece of regulation that many businesses aren't yet aware of. The Biometric Processing Privacy Code came into force on 3 November 2025, with a compliance deadline of **3 August 2026**.

### What Counts as Biometric Data?

| Biometric Type | Examples |
|---------------|----------|
| Facial recognition | Security cameras with face matching, photo ID verification |
| Fingerprints | Fingerprint scanners for time tracking or access |
| Voiceprints | Voice authentication on phone systems |
| Iris scans | Eye-based identification systems |
| Gait analysis | Movement pattern recognition |

### What the Code Requires

- If you use any technology that processes biometric information, you need to comply.
- The Code **prohibits inferring sensitive information** from biometrics — you can't use facial recognition to try to determine someone's ethnicity, gender, or emotional state, except for genuine safety purposes.
- You must have a lawful purpose for collecting biometric data and appropriate safeguards in place.
- You need to tell people when you're collecting their biometric data and why.

### The Foodstuffs Case Study

This is a real NZ example of what can go wrong. Foodstuffs North Island deployed facial recognition technology (FRT) across its stores, scanning over **225 million faces** to identify potential shoplifters. The Privacy Commissioner launched an inquiry, raising serious concerns about proportionality — was mass facial scanning of every customer an appropriate response to retail theft?

The case highlights a key principle: just because AI technology exists doesn't mean you should use it. The scale of data collection needs to be proportionate to the problem you're solving.

> **Important:** If you use any AI system that involves cameras, voice recognition, or biometric identification, check whether the Biometric Processing Privacy Code applies to you. The compliance deadline is 3 August 2026, but you should start preparing now.

### Does This Affect Your Business?

You might be affected if you use:

- Smart security cameras with facial detection
- Biometric time clocks (fingerprint or face scan)
- Voice-based customer verification
- Any AI tool that analyses faces, voices, or physical characteristics

If none of these apply to you, you're in the clear — but it's worth knowing about as AI capabilities expand.

## Insurance Options

Insurance doesn't prevent problems, but it protects your business when they happen. As AI becomes more common, insurers are paying closer attention to how businesses use it.

| Insurance Type | What It Covers | AI Relevance |
|---------------|---------------|--------------|
| **Professional Indemnity** | Negligence, errors, and omissions in professional services — including Privacy Act failures | Covers you if AI-assisted work causes a client loss or a privacy breach |
| **Cyber Liability** | Data breach costs — notification, forensic investigation, legal fees, business interruption | Covers the fallout if customer data is exposed through an AI tool |
| **Directors & Officers (D&O)** | Personal liability of directors for governance decisions | Relevant if board/leadership decisions about AI deployment lead to harm |
| **Management Liability** | Broader coverage for management decisions and employment practices | Covers claims arising from AI-related workplace decisions |

### What Insurers Are Asking

Insurance companies are increasingly asking about AI use when they underwrite policies. You might be asked:

- Do you use AI in your business operations?
- What AI tools do you use and for what purposes?
- Do you have an AI policy for staff?
- How do you protect customer data when using AI?
- Have you done a risk assessment for your AI use?

Having good answers to these questions — and having a documented AI policy — can help with both getting coverage and keeping premiums reasonable.

> **Tip:** When you next renew your business insurance, ask your broker specifically about AI-related coverage. Make sure your policy doesn't exclude AI-related claims, and consider whether you need cyber liability insurance if you don't already have it.

## Putting It All Together

Here's a practical approach to managing AI risk in your business:

1. **Audit your current AI use.** What tools are being used? What data goes into them?
2. **Implement basic security practices.** Business accounts, MFA, data handling rules.
3. **Create or update your AI policy.** (See the previous lesson for a template.)
4. **Check whether biometric regulations apply to you.** If so, plan for compliance by August 2026.
5. **Review your insurance coverage.** Talk to your broker about AI-related risks.
6. **Stay informed.** The regulatory landscape is evolving — check in on new developments at least quarterly.

## Key Takeaways

- One in three NZ SMEs has experienced a cyber-attack. AI creates new pathways for data exposure, especially through free tools and shadow AI.
- Basic security practices make a big difference: use business accounts, enable MFA, don't paste confidential data into free AI tools, and anonymise data where possible.
- The Biometric Processing Privacy Code 2025 applies to facial recognition, fingerprints, voiceprints, and more. Compliance deadline is 3 August 2026.
- Review your insurance to ensure AI-related risks are covered. Professional Indemnity, Cyber Liability, and D&O insurance are the key types to consider.
- Insurers are now asking about AI use during underwriting — having a documented policy and good practices will help.
