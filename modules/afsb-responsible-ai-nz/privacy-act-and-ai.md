# Privacy Act 2020 and AI

You don't need to be a lawyer to get this right — but you do need to understand the basics. Every time you use an AI tool with customer information, the Privacy Act 2020 is in play. This lesson breaks down what that means in plain English.

## When Does the Privacy Act Apply to AI?

The short answer: whenever personal information is involved. Personal information is any data that can identify a real person — names, email addresses, phone numbers, physical addresses, purchase history, IP addresses, even photos.

So if you're pasting a customer email into ChatGPT to draft a reply, or uploading a spreadsheet of client details into an AI tool for analysis, the Privacy Act applies. It doesn't matter that AI is doing the work instead of a person.

> **Important:** The Privacy Act applies to how you *collect*, *store*, *use*, and *share* personal information — regardless of whether a human or an AI is processing it.

## The 13 Information Privacy Principles — What Matters Most

The Privacy Act has 13 Information Privacy Principles (IPPs). You don't need to memorise all of them, but four are particularly important when you're using AI.

| Principle | What It Means for AI Use |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **IPP 1 — Purpose** | Only collect personal information for a lawful, clear purpose connected to your business. Don't feed AI data "just to see what happens." |
| **IPP 5 — Storage & Security** | Keep personal information secure. That means understanding where your AI tool stores data and who can access it. |
| **IPP 8 — Accuracy** | If AI produces analysis or profiles based on personal data, you're responsible for making sure the output is accurate before you act on it. |
| **IPP 11 — Disclosure** | Don't share personal information with third parties unless your customer would reasonably expect it. Sending data to an AI platform counts as disclosure. |

## IPP 12 — Cross-Border Data Transfers

This is the big one for AI. When you use ChatGPT, Claude, Google Gemini, or Microsoft Copilot, your data typically gets processed on servers overseas — usually in the United States.

Under IPP 12, you can send personal information overseas, but only if the overseas recipient is subject to privacy protections that are comparable to NZ's. In practice, here's what this means:

- **Read the AI tool's privacy policy.** Where is data processed? What protections do they offer?
- **Use business/enterprise accounts** where available — these typically have stronger data protection agreements than free tiers.
- **Get consent where needed.** If you're sending sensitive customer data overseas through an AI tool and the protections aren't comparable, you should tell customers and get their agreement.
- **Consider NZ-hosted options.** AWS launched its NZ region in 2025, meaning some tools can now process data within New Zealand.

> **Tip:** Most major AI platforms (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft) have data processing agreements for business accounts that address cross-border requirements. Check before you sign up.

## IPP 3A — Coming 1 May 2026

A new privacy obligation is on the way. From 1 May 2026, if you collect personal information about someone from a *third party* (not directly from them), you'll need to tell that person you have their data.

Why does this matter for AI? Because AI tools can aggregate, enrich, and cross-reference data from multiple sources. If your AI system pulls in data about a person from a source other than that person themselves, you'll have a new obligation to notify them.

**Start preparing now:** Review how your AI tools collect and combine data. If any personal information comes from sources other than the individual, build a notification process.

## Do a Privacy Impact Assessment

The Privacy Commissioner recommends doing a Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) before you deploy any AI system that handles personal information. This doesn't have to be complicated. It's simply a structured way to think through:

- What personal information does this AI tool process?
- Where is the data stored and processed?
- Who has access to it?
- What could go wrong?
- How will you mitigate those risks?

For a small business, this might be a one-page document. The point is to think it through *before* you start, not after something goes wrong.

## Before You Paste Customer Data Into AI

Here's a practical checklist you can use every time you're about to put personal information into an AI tool:

- [ ] **Do I actually need to use real customer data?** Could I anonymise it or use dummy data instead?
- [ ] **Does my customer know their data might be processed by AI?** Would they reasonably expect this?
- [ ] **Am I using a business account** with proper data protection terms, or a free personal account?
- [ ] **Where will this data be stored and processed?** Am I comfortable with that location?
- [ ] **Could this data be used to train the AI model?** Many free-tier tools use your inputs for training — business accounts usually don't.
- [ ] **Am I only sharing what's needed?** Remove any personal details that aren't necessary for the task.
- [ ] **Is the AI output accurate?** Have I checked it before acting on it or sending it to the customer?

> **Warning:** Free versions of AI tools often use your inputs to train their models. That means your customer data could end up influencing responses to other users. Always check the tool's data usage policy, and use business accounts for anything involving personal information.

## What to Tell Your Customers

Transparency builds trust. Consider updating your privacy policy or privacy notice to mention that you use AI tools in your business. You don't need pages of legal text — something like this is a good start:

*"We may use AI-powered tools to help us serve you better, such as drafting communications or analysing trends. When we do, we take steps to protect your personal information in line with the Privacy Act 2020, including using business-grade tools with appropriate data protection agreements."*

If you're unsure, the Office of the Privacy Commissioner has free guidance on their website at privacy.org.nz.

## Key Takeaways

- The Privacy Act 2020 applies every time AI processes personal information — there are no AI exemptions.
- Focus on four key principles: Purpose, Storage/Security, Accuracy, and Disclosure.
- Cross-border data transfers (IPP 12) are relevant to virtually every cloud-based AI tool. Know where your data goes.
- A new notification requirement (IPP 3A) takes effect on 1 May 2026 — prepare now.
- Do a Privacy Impact Assessment before deploying AI, even a simple one.
- Use business accounts, anonymise data where possible, and be transparent with customers.
