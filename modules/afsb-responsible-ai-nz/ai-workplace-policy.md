# Creating Your AI Workplace Policy

Here's a stat that should get your attention: only 48% of NZ businesses using AI have staff policies in place. Meanwhile, 52% identify "shadow AI" — staff using unapproved AI tools without the business knowing — as a problem. If you don't have a policy, your team is almost certainly already using AI. The question is whether they're using it safely.

## Why You Need a Policy (Even If You're a Small Team)

You might think a formal AI policy is something only big corporates need. But consider this:

- **Your staff are already using AI.** Even if you haven't introduced it officially, people are using ChatGPT on their phones to draft emails, summarise documents, and solve problems. Without guidance, they might be putting sensitive business or customer data at risk.
- **You're legally responsible.** Under the Privacy Act, you're accountable for how personal information is handled in your business — including by AI tools your staff use.
- **Consistency matters.** Without a policy, one team member might be careful while another pastes client files straight into a free AI tool.
- **It sets expectations.** A clear policy tells your team "we support using AI, and here's how to do it well."

> **Tip:** A good AI policy isn't about restricting your team — it's about empowering them to use AI confidently while protecting the business.

## What to Include in Your Policy

Your AI policy doesn't need to be a 20-page document. For a small business, one to two pages is ideal. Here are the essential elements.

### 1. Approved Tools

Be specific about which AI tools your business has approved for use. This might include:

- AI assistants (e.g., ChatGPT Business, Claude Team, Microsoft Copilot)
- AI features within existing tools (e.g., Xero's JAX, Canva AI)
- Any industry-specific AI tools you've vetted

Make it clear that using unapproved AI tools for business purposes is not permitted without checking first.

### 2. Data Rules — What Can and Can't Go Into AI

This is the most important section. Be very clear about what information staff can and cannot enter into AI tools.

| Data Type | Can It Go Into AI? | Notes |
|-----------|--------------------|-------|
| Public information (your website content, published articles) | Yes | Low risk |
| Internal documents (meeting notes, process docs) | Usually yes | Use approved tools only |
| Customer names and contact details | With caution | Anonymise where possible |
| Financial records | No | Use dedicated financial tools instead |
| Health or sensitive personal information | No | Too high-risk for general AI tools |
| Passwords, API keys, credentials | Never | Absolute rule |
| Customer files or confidential documents | No | Unless using an approved, enterprise tool |

### 3. Quality Checks

AI makes mistakes — sometimes confidently. Your policy should require:

- **Human review of all AI outputs** before they go to customers or are published
- **Fact-checking of claims and statistics** — AI frequently fabricates data
- **Proofreading for tone and accuracy** — especially for customer-facing content
- **Disclosure where appropriate** — be honest with clients about AI use when relevant

### 4. Accountability

Make it clear who's responsible:

- The person who uses the AI tool is responsible for checking the output
- Managers are responsible for ensuring their team follows the policy
- The business owner is ultimately accountable for compliance

### 5. Regular Review

AI changes fast. Commit to reviewing your policy at least every six months — or whenever you adopt a new AI tool.

## Sample One-Page AI Policy Framework

Here's a template you can adapt for your business. Replace the bracketed sections with your own details.

---

**[Business Name] — AI Use Policy**

**Purpose:** This policy guides how our team uses AI tools to work smarter while protecting our customers, our data, and our reputation.

**Approved AI Tools:**
- [List your approved tools, e.g., ChatGPT Business, Claude Team, Canva AI, Xero JAX]
- Other AI tools must be approved by [name/role] before use for business purposes.

**Data Rules:**
- DO use AI for: drafting content, brainstorming, research on public topics, summarising internal notes (via approved tools)
- DO NOT put into AI tools: customer personal information (unless anonymised), financial records, passwords, confidential client files, health information
- Always use our business accounts, not personal or free accounts

**Quality Standards:**
- All AI-generated content must be reviewed by a human before being sent to customers or published
- Check facts, statistics, and claims — AI makes things up
- Disclose AI use to clients when it's relevant to the service they're receiving

**Responsibility:**
- Each team member is responsible for following this policy
- If you're unsure whether something is appropriate, ask [name/role] before proceeding

**Review:** This policy will be reviewed every [6 months] or when we adopt new AI tools.

*Last updated: [date]*

---

## Introducing AI to Your Team

Having a policy is one thing. Getting your team on board is another. Here's how to do it without creating resistance.

### Start With the Benefits

Lead with what's in it for them. AI can take the boring, repetitive parts of their job off their plate. Frame it as a tool that makes their work easier, not a threat to their role.

### Give People Time to Learn

Not everyone picks up new technology at the same speed. Provide training opportunities and allow time for experimentation. Consider:

- A team workshop where everyone tries an AI tool together
- Pairing an AI-enthusiastic team member with someone more cautious
- Setting aside "AI experiment time" where people can explore without pressure

### Make It Safe to Ask Questions

Create an environment where people feel comfortable saying "I'm not sure about this" or "I accidentally put something into AI that I shouldn't have." Punishing mistakes discourages transparency and pushes AI use underground — exactly the shadow AI problem you're trying to avoid.

> **Important:** The goal is making AI a normal, well-managed part of how your team works — not creating fear around it. People who feel supported will use AI more responsibly than people who feel surveilled.

### Set Expectations for AI Literacy

Different roles need different levels of AI understanding:

| Role | AI Literacy Level Needed |
|------|--------------------------|
| Business owner/manager | Policy, risk, strategy — understand what AI can do and where the risks are |
| Customer-facing staff | Approved tools, data rules, quality checking — know how to use AI safely |
| Marketing/content roles | Prompt engineering, fact-checking, copyright awareness |
| Admin/operations | Workflow automation, data handling, approved tool use |
| All staff | Basic awareness of what AI is, your business policy, and when to ask for help |

Only 24% of NZ workers have received any AI training. Even basic training puts your team ahead of most.

## Keep It Current

AI moves fast. A policy written today might be partly outdated in six months. Build in a review cycle:

- **Every 6 months:** Review and update the policy
- **When you adopt a new tool:** Update the approved tools list
- **When laws change:** Update data rules (e.g., IPP 3A taking effect May 2026)
- **After an incident:** Review what happened and update the policy to prevent recurrence

## Key Takeaways

- Over half of NZ businesses using AI have no staff policy — and shadow AI is a growing problem.
- Your policy doesn't need to be long. One to two pages covering approved tools, data rules, quality checks, and accountability is enough.
- Use the sample framework in this lesson as a starting point and customise it for your business.
- Introduce AI to your team by leading with benefits, providing training, and making it safe to ask questions.
- Review your policy at least every six months — the technology and the law are both moving fast.
