# Skills Gap Analysis

Here's the stat that should grab your attention: 43% of NZ businesses that aren't using AI say the main reason is a lack of expertise. Not cost. Not lack of interest. They just don't know how to get started — and neither do their teams.

Meanwhile, only 24% of Kiwi workers have received any AI training at all. That's a massive gap between the tools that are available and the skills people have to use them. The good news? The skills your team actually needs are much more accessible than you might think.

## What Skills Do You Actually Need?

Let's clear up the biggest misconception first: **your team does not need to learn coding, data science, or machine learning.** For the vast majority of small businesses, the skills that matter are:

| Skill | What It Means | Who Needs It |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| **Prompt writing** | Knowing how to ask AI tools clear questions to get useful answers | Everyone who'll use AI |
| **Critical thinking** | Being able to evaluate whether AI output is accurate and appropriate | Everyone who'll use AI |
| **Basic data literacy** | Understanding what data you have and what it means | Team leads, managers |
| **Tool confidence** | Comfort with trying new software and learning by doing | Everyone |
| **AI awareness** | Understanding what AI can and can't do, plus ethical considerations | Everyone |

> **Important:** The number one skill for using AI effectively is prompt writing — knowing how to give clear, specific instructions. It's like being a good delegator. If you can brief a new staff member clearly, you can learn to prompt AI.

## Assessing Your Team's Current Level

Before you plan any training, you need to know where everyone's at. Here's a simple framework for assessing digital literacy and AI readiness across your team:

### Level 1: Digital Beginner
- Uses basic software (email, Word) but struggles with anything new
- Uncomfortable with change
- Hasn't used any AI tools
- Needs structured, step-by-step guidance

### Level 2: Digital Competent
- Comfortable with everyday business tools (Xero, Google Workspace, etc.)
- Can learn new software with some support
- Might have tried ChatGPT once or twice out of curiosity
- Open to learning but unsure where to start

### Level 3: Digital Confident
- Picks up new tools quickly
- Already experimenting with AI in some way
- Can troubleshoot basic tech issues
- Could help others learn

### Level 4: Digital Champion
- Actively seeks out new tools and techniques
- Uses AI regularly and effectively
- Understands both the potential and the limitations
- Could train and mentor others

> **Tip:** Don't assess people based on their age or job title. Some of the most enthusiastic AI adopters are experienced business owners who see the time-saving potential, while some younger team members might be surprisingly resistant to change.

## Running a Quick Skills Audit

Here's a practical way to assess your team in 15 minutes:

**Ask each team member to rate themselves 1–5 on these statements:**

1. I'm comfortable using cloud-based tools (Google Workspace, Xero, etc.)
2. I can find and evaluate information online quickly
3. I've used an AI tool like ChatGPT, Claude, or Copilot
4. I could write a clear prompt to get useful output from an AI tool
5. I can tell when AI output is good quality vs. when it's wrong or misleading
6. I understand basic data privacy (what we should and shouldn't share with AI tools)
7. I'm willing to change how I work if a better tool comes along
8. I could teach a colleague how to use a new digital tool

**Average score of 1–2:** This person needs foundational digital skills first.
**Average score of 3–4:** Ready for AI-specific training.
**Average score of 4–5:** Potential AI champion — start with them.

## Upskilling vs Hiring vs Outsourcing

When you find gaps in your team's capabilities, you've got three options. Here's how they compare:

| Factor | Upskilling | Hiring | Outsourcing |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Cost** | $1,200–3,000 per person | $80K–120K+ salary | $150–300/hr for specialists |
| **Timeline** | Weeks to months | 3–6 months to recruit, 6–12 months to full productivity | Immediate |
| **Institutional knowledge** | Preserved and enhanced | Takes time to build | They never fully get it |
| **Ongoing dependency** | Low — skills stay with your team | Medium — person could leave | High — you rely on them |
| **Best for** | Core AI skills your team will use daily | A dedicated AI/digital role you'll need long-term | One-off projects or specialist expertise |

> **Important:** For most small businesses, upskilling your existing team is the best first move. It's cheaper, faster, and means your people — who already understand your business — become your AI capability.

## The "Community of Multipliers" Approach

Here's a strategy that works brilliantly for small teams: instead of training everyone at once, train a few people well and let them teach the rest.

**How it works:**

1. **Identify 1–2 "AI champions"** — your most digitally confident and enthusiastic team members
2. **Invest in proper training** for them — send them to a workshop, get them a ChatGPT or Claude subscription, give them time to experiment
3. **Set them loose** — let them find AI solutions to real problems in your business
4. **Have them share what they learn** — regular 15-minute show-and-tells with the team
5. **Expand gradually** — as interest grows, train the next group

This works because people learn better from colleagues they trust than from external trainers. And it creates genuine enthusiasm rather than forced adoption.

> **Warning:** Don't force AI on team members who are anxious about it. Fear of being replaced is real. Be clear that AI is about removing tedious tasks, not removing people. Frame it as "this will take the boring stuff off your plate" rather than "this will do your job."

## Addressing the Fear Factor

54% of organisations cite resistance to change as the number one obstacle to AI adoption. For small businesses, this often shows up as:

- **"AI will take my job"** — Address this directly. Be honest about what will and won't change.
- **"I'm too old/not techy enough"** — Prompt writing is a communication skill, not a technical one. Anyone who can write a clear email can learn it.
- **"What if I break something?"** — Create a safe space to experiment. Use AI tools on non-critical tasks first.
- **"We don't have time to learn"** — Start with just 15 minutes a day. The time investment pays back quickly.

## Key Takeaways

- **Lack of expertise is the #1 barrier** to AI adoption — but the skills needed are accessible, not technical
- **Prompt writing and critical thinking** are the core skills, not coding or data science
- **Assess your team's current level** with a simple self-rating exercise before planning training
- **Start with 1–2 AI champions** and let them teach the rest — the "Community of Multipliers" approach is the most effective strategy for small teams
